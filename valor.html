Field Marshal Sam Manekshaw

"Gentlemen, I have arrived and there are three things I'd like to make clear:
I have not eaten curry since 1935; I speak English; and I am here to lead you."

Field Marshal Sam Manekshaw, born on April 3, 1914, was a towering figure in the annals of the Indian military. Commissioned into the British Indian Army in 1934, he went on to play an instrumental role in shaping the destiny of an independent India. His remarkable career spanned pre- and post-independence periods, witnessing the transformation of the British Indian Army into the formidable Indian Army. Manekshaw's leadership prowess became particularly evident during the 1971 Indo-Pak War. As the Chief of the Army Staff, his strategic brilliance and charismatic command were pivotal in India's resounding victory, leading to the creation of Bangladesh. Affectionately known as "Sam Bahadur," his troops revered him for his courage and wisdom.
His leadership style was characterized by pragmatism, humor, and a genuine concern for his soldiers. His nickname, "Sam Bahadur," encapsulated the essence of a brave and valiant leader who inspired confidence and loyalty. Despite the challenges of war, Manekshaw's wit remained intact, and his famous retort during the conflict, "I am always ready, sweetie," showcased his unyielding spirit.
Post-retirement, Field Marshal Manekshaw continued to be an influential figure, providing insights on military and political matters. His legacy extends beyond the battlefield; it encompasses the values of integrity, leadership, and indomitable spirit. Sam Manekshaw's numerous accolades, including the Padma Bhushan and Padma Vibhushan, speak to the nation's recognition of his exceptional service. He passed away on June 27, 2008, leaving behind a legacy that continues to inspire generations of military personnel and civilians alike, embodying the epitome of a true national hero.

Major Somnath Sharma

"When you have to choose between your country and your life, there is only one choice—choose your country, and you will live to serve it."

Major Somnath Sharma, born on January 31, 1923, in Himachal Pradesh, India, emerged as a beacon of valor and sacrifice in the annals of the Indian military. Commissioned into the 4th Battalion, Kumaon Regiment, during World War II, he etched his name in history during the First Kashmir War in 1947-48. Leading his troops with unwavering resolve, Major Sharma played a pivotal role in the defense of the Srinagar Airport, facing overwhelming odds against tribal invaders backed by the Pakistani Army. On November 3, 1947, he and his men braved heavy enemy fire, displaying unparalleled courage. Severely wounded yet undeterred, Major Somnath Sharma continued to inspire his troops until he succumbed to his injuries, becoming the first recipient of the Param Vir Chakra, India's highest military decoration for bravery.
Major Sharma's legacy transcends his heroic actions on the battlefield. His sacrifice is a poignant reminder of the selfless dedication and indomitable spirit that defines the Indian armed forces. The Major Somnath Sharma Memorial Lecture, organized annually by the Indian Army, pays tribute to his exemplary service. Major Somnath Sharma's life and sacrifice continue to inspire generations, symbolizing the highest ideals of duty, honor, and courage. In the tapestry of India's military history, Major Somnath Sharma stands as a symbol of resilience and sacrifice, an immortal figure whose gallantry continues to resonate as a source of inspiration for the nation.


Captain Vikram Batra

“We live by chance, we love by choice, we kill by profession.”

Captain Vikram Batra, an emblem of bravery and valor, was born on September 9, 1974, in Palampur, Himachal Pradesh, India. Commissioned into the 13 JAK Rifles of the Indian Army, he rose to prominence during the Kargil War in 1999. His indomitable spirit and courage in the face of adversity became evident during the crucial battles to recapture Point 4875, famously known as "Point 4875, Tiger Hill," where he uttered the now-iconic words, "Yeh Dil Maange More!" (This heart craves more!). His leadership and fearlessness in the battlefield played a pivotal role in India's success during the Kargil conflict.
On July 7, 1999, during the final assault on Point 4875, Captain Vikram Batra made the ultimate sacrifice for his country. His selfless act of heroism and dedication to the mission earned him the Param Vir Chakra posthumously, making him a symbol of inspiration for generations to come. His famous quote, "Either I will come back after hoisting the Tricolor, or I will come back wrapped in it. But I will be back for sure," encapsulates his unwavering commitment to the cause and the depth of his patriotism.
Captain Vikram Batra's legacy extends beyond the battlefield. His life and sacrifice continue to be celebrated in books, documentaries, and films, including the Bollywood movie "Shershaah." His memory serves as a poignant reminder of the sacrifices made by the brave men and women of the Indian armed forces. Captain Vikram Batra remains immortalized as a national hero, embodying the ideals of courage, duty, and love for the motherland.


Lt. Arun Khetarpal

“No Sir, I will not abandon my tank. My gun is still working, and I will get these bastards.”

Lieutenant Arun Khetarpal, born on October 14, 1950, in Pune, Maharashtra, India, etched his name in the annals of military history through an act of unparalleled bravery during the 1971 Indo-Pak War. Commissioned into the 17 Poona Horse regiment, Khetarpal's defining moment came during the Battle of Basantar. On December 16, 1971, his troop faced an overwhelming enemy force, and despite being outnumbered and severely outnumbered, Khetarpal displayed extraordinary courage. In the face of imminent danger, he refused to abandon his damaged tank, choosing to fight on and inspire his men. His final communication, "No Sir, I will not abandon my tank. My Main's damaged, and I've got a crew injured. But I'm going to get the next tank to fire on the enemy," epitomized his unwavering commitment to duty. Lt. Arun Khetarpal's gallantry earned him the Param Vir Chakra posthumously, making him the youngest recipient of India's highest military honor. His sacrifice stands as a symbol of indomitable spirit, selflessness, and the ultimate dedication to the defense of the nation. The legacy of Lieutenant Arun Khetarpal continues to inspire generations of military personnel and civilians alike, underscoring the timeless values of valor and sacrifice that define the heroes of the Indian armed forces. His story is a poignant reminder of the extraordinary sacrifices made by individuals like him, ensuring that their memory remains eternally embedded in the fabric of India's rich military heritage.


Rifleman Jaswant Singh Rawat

The Man Who Saved Arunachal Pradesh From The Chinese

Rifleman Jaswant Singh Rawat, a name etched in the annals of Indian military history, rose to prominence during the 1962 Sino-Indian War. Stationed at an outpost in the strategically vital Battle of Rezang La, Rawat displayed unparalleled valor and determination. Despite being vastly outnumbered by the Chinese forces, he, along with two comrades, held their ground with unwavering resolve. When his comrades fell, Rifleman Rawat continued the solitary defense of the post. His legendary act included not only defending against the enemy but also tending to the wounded and maintaining the post's crucial machine gun. The Chinese, awed by his relentless courage, believed that he was supported by a much larger force and, fearing heavy losses, retreated. Rifleman Jaswant Singh Rawat's sacrifice was profound, as he fought to his last breath, refusing to surrender or retreat. His gallant stand and indomitable spirit earned him admiration and respect.
Jaswant Singh Rawat's story goes beyond the battlefield. Locals in the area, touched by his bravery, built a shrine in his honor at the site of his last stand. The legend of Jaswant Singh Rawat, who continued to guard the post even in death, is a testament to the enduring spirit of sacrifice that defines the Indian armed forces. The Indian government posthumously awarded him the Maha Vir Chakra, recognizing his exceptional bravery. Rifleman Jaswant Singh Rawat remains an enduring symbol of courage, patriotism, and the unyielding determination to defend the sovereignty of the nation, and his legacy continues to inspire generations of Indians.


Squadron Leader Rakesh Sharma

"If I were part of Gaganyaan, I could have been sure that for the next few years, there would have been a second space mission and a third space mission....."

Squadron Leader Rakesh Sharma, born on January 13, 1949, in Patiala, Punjab, is an iconic figure in the history of the Indian Air Force (IAF) and space exploration. A test pilot with the IAF, Sharma was selected as one of two pilots to represent India in the Intercosmos program, a Soviet space mission. On April 2, 1984, aboard the Soviet spacecraft Soyuz T-11, he embarked on a historic journey, becoming the first Indian citizen to travel in space.
During his mission, when he was asked by the then Prime Minister of India, Indira Gandhi, about how India looked from space, Sharma responded with the now-famous words, "Saare Jahan Se Achha" . This eloquent expression not only reflected the beauty of the Earth but also symbolized the unity and diversity of the nation. Squadron Leader Rakesh Sharma's achievements extend beyond his historic spaceflight. He served in the IAF, rising to the rank of Wing Commander, and received the Ashoka Chakra, India's highest peacetime gallantry award, for his outstanding contributions. Post his military career, Sharma has continued to inspire and contribute to the field of aerospace and technology.
His journey remains a source of inspiration for aspiring astronauts and a testament to India's capabilities in space exploration. Squadron Leader Rakesh Sharma's legacy goes beyond national borders, encapsulating the spirit of human endeavor and the pursuit of knowledge that defines the exploration of the cosmos.


Air Marshal Arjan Singh

"Leadership is not just about giving energy... it's unleashing other people's energy."

Air Marshal Arjan Singh, born on April 15, 1919, in Lyallpur, British India (now Faisalabad, Pakistan), stands as an iconic figure in the history of the Indian Air Force (IAF). Commissioned into the Royal Indian Air Force in 1938, he played a pivotal role in shaping the IAF's destiny. Notably, during the 1965 Indo-Pak War, as the Chief of the Air Staff, Arjan Singh's leadership and strategic acumen were instrumental in the IAF's success. His dynamic approach and quick decision-making skills contributed significantly to India's air superiority during the conflict. In recognition of his exceptional service, he was promoted to the rank of Air Chief Marshal, becoming the first and only officer of the IAF to achieve this rank. Subsequently, in 2002, he was honored with the rank of Marshal of the Indian Air Force, the highest honorary rank, in acknowledgment of his outstanding contributions.
Air Marshal Arjan Singh's legacy extends beyond his military achievements. He was known for his humility, integrity, and commitment to the welfare of his personnel. His leadership philosophy emphasized unleashing the energy of others, reflecting a collaborative and empowering approach. Even in his later years, he continued to be a respected voice in defense and strategic matters.
Air Marshal Arjan Singh's remarkable career spanned several decades, witnessing the transformation of the IAF into a modern and potent force. His indomitable spirit, dedication to duty, and visionary leadership continue to inspire the Indian armed forces and the nation. He passed away on September 16, 2017, leaving behind a legacy that epitomizes the highest ideals of military service and leadership in the defense of the nation.


Wing Commander
Abhinandan Varthaman

It doesn't matter what the other side is saying. I see no reason for us to escalate the situation. If they come in our airspace, the boys (IAF pilots) will take care of it."

Wing Commander Abhinandan Varthaman, born on June 21, 1983, emerged as a national hero and symbol of valor for India in the aftermath of the 2019 Balakot airstrike. A distinguished pilot of the Indian Air Force (IAF), Abhinandan, flying a MiG-21 Bison, engaged with Pakistani aircraft during an aerial skirmish in the aftermath of the airstrike. After successfully downing an enemy fighter, his aircraft was hit, leading to his capture by Pakistani forces. During his captivity, Abhinandan displayed remarkable composure and adherence to military protocols, refusing to divulge sensitive information. His release, facilitated by diplomatic efforts, was met with jubilation across India.Abhinandan's calm demeanor under intense pressure and his dedication to duty became exemplary traits that resonated with the nation. His stoic response, "I'm not supposed to tell you that," when questioned by his captors, became a symbol of military discipline and resilience. Upon his return to India, he received a hero's welcome, and his courage was acknowledged with the Vir Chakra, one of India's gallantry awards.
Wing Commander Abhinandan Varthaman's story transcends the boundaries of military service; it embodies the indomitable spirit of a soldier, his sacrifice, and the unity that his return symbolized for the nation.
